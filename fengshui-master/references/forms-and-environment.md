## Form-School Priority

Form analysis is often the most practical first layer because it depends on observable conditions. Analyze from macro to micro:

1. Regional setting: terrain, water, road network, surrounding density.
2. Site: approach, open space, containment, noise, exposure.
3. Building: shape, entrance, orientation, neighboring structures.
4. Unit: door, windows, circulation, key rooms.
5. Person: bed, desk, stove, seating, daily movement.

## Four Emblems

The four-emblem model is a heuristic for support and exposure.

| Emblem | Directional image | Spatial reading |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Black Tortoise | rear support | higher/stable backing, protection, privacy |
| Red Phoenix | front openness | clear view, approach space, breathing room |
| Green Dragon | left support/activity | supportive movement or slightly stronger left side |
| White Tiger | right support/control | calmer, lower, less aggressive right side |

Use as image logic, not rigid geography. In urban apartments, "rear support" may mean a solid wall behind bed or desk rather than an actual mountain.

## Mountains, Water, Roads

Traditional landscape readings often treat mountains as support and water as movement/wealth circulation. In cities, buildings can act like mountains and roads can act like water.

Favorable tendencies:

- Curving, embracing roads or water that slow and gather.
- Clear but not overly exposed approach to the entrance.
- Stable rear support and moderate side protection.
- Clean, visible, usable open space before the entrance.

Unfavorable tendencies:

- Fast straight road or corridor directly striking a door/window.
- Water, road, or traffic rushing away from the site without containment.
- Rear exposure, steep drop, or unsupported back.
- Confusing access, hidden entrance, dead-end stagnation, or harsh noise.

## External Sha Qi

Sha qi means harsh, cutting, rushing, oppressive, or destabilizing influence. Translate it into observable issues first.

Common forms:

- Direct road, alley, corridor, or stair rush toward a door.
- Sharp corner, roofline, pole, tower, sign, or edge aimed at a window/door.
- T-junction pressure.
- Door aligned with elevator, stair, long corridor, or another door.
- Strong noise, glare, wind tunnel, exhaust, odor, dampness, trash, or clutter.
- Oversized structure pressing close to the site.

Typical responses:

- Increase buffer: plants, screens, curtains, vestibule, gate, distance.
- Redirect flow: angled mat, lighting, signage, furniture placement.
- Improve threshold: clear entry, visible number/sign, clean path, balanced light.
- Use practical mitigation first: acoustic sealing, ventilation, maintenance, drainage.

## Building and Unit Form

Evaluate:

- Regularity: square/rectangular forms are easier to stabilize than fragmented, missing-corner, or heavily protruding forms.
- Center: avoid heavy clutter, toilets, stairs, or blocked circulation at the center when possible.
- Proportion: overly narrow, long, triangular, or L-shaped plans need attention to circulation and use.
- Light and air: stale, dark, damp, or overheated spaces often matter more than symbolic direction.
- Vertical pressure: beams, low ceilings, stairs, and heavy overhead storage can create discomfort.

Missing-corner interpretation:

- Identify the missing sector using a compass-based plan only if bearings are reliable.
- Do not overstate. First ask whether the missing area causes practical problems: poor use, weak daylight, awkward circulation, or underused zones.
- Remedies can include lighting, mirrors where safe, plants, art, furniture activation, or functional compensation in another room.

## Entrance and Ming Tang

The entrance is the main qi mouth in many methods.

Good entrance qualities:

- Easy to find.
- Clean, bright, safe, and unobstructed.
- Has a small gathering area rather than immediate compression.
- Door opens fully and works well.
- Path from outside to inside is legible.

Warnings:

- Door blocked by clutter or furniture.
- Door opens directly into stair, toilet, mirror, sharp corner, or long corridor.
- Entrance is dark, dirty, noisy, or hard to identify.
- Front and back openings align so movement rushes straight through.

## Interior Flow

Read flow by walking the route:

1. Entry arrival.
2. First view.
3. Main circulation path.
4. Places where flow speeds up, crashes, leaks, or stagnates.
5. Resting places: bed, sofa, desk, dining table.

Common issues:

- Long straight corridors accelerate movement.
- Doors directly opposite create conflict or leakage symbolism.
- Bed or desk in direct line with door can feel exposed.
- Seating without back support can reduce perceived stability.
- Clutter at corners or thresholds stagnates use.

## Modern Environmental Checks

Always include practical checks when relevant:

- Fire exits and egress must remain clear.
- Ventilation, daylight, moisture, mold, electrical load, and structural safety override symbolic remedies.
- Water features need maintenance, spill protection, child/pet safety, and electrical separation.
- Plants need light and care; dead or struggling plants are worse than none.
- Mirrors should not create glare, disorientation, privacy issues, or nighttime discomfort.
- Workplace advice should support ergonomics, visibility, acoustics, and actual productivity.
